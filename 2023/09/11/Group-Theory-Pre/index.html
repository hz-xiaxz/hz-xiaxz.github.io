Groups:definition and examples

1.1 Symmetry and group

Symmetry is to discribe how an object keeps invariant under a certain transformation, like rotating a circle around the center. Sometimes, after a transformation, the property turns exact the opposite, we will call it antisymmetry, like a reflective transformation.

There’s no doubt that the transformation has solid connection with the inner property of the object. Objects can be really different, like a n-D hypercube and a normal 2-D square, however, they have the same symmetry under reflective transformation, which helps us to use known knowledge to solve unfamiliar problems.

What’s taken into our consideration is of course the set of the transformations or operations, and that thoughts drive to the notion of group.

A group is a set on which we are given a binary operation which behaves much like ordinary mulitiplication;that is, we are given a map of $G\times G\to G$ (often $\times $ is ommitted) sending the pair $(p, q)$ into $pq$, which satisfies:

  1. closure(for sure)
  2. associativity
  3. having an identity element $I$
  4. having the existence of an inverse for each element.

If the operation is commute, then the group is called abelian, if not, it is called nonabelian

1.2 Examples of the groups

  1. $\mathbb{Z} \text{ or } \mathbb{R}$ under additon generates a group, its identity element is $0$, the inverse of $u$ is $-u$.

  2. $\mathbb{R}^*$ under mulitiplication generates a group, its identity element is $1$, the inverse of $u$ is $\frac{1}{u}$.

  3. The addition of integers mod $N$ generates a group, its elements are ${0, 1, \cdots , N-1}$.
    That’s the typical defination of the mathematical operation ‘mod’, but if we define the elements to be ${-1, 0, 1, \cdots , N-2}$, it is still the same group.
    Take $N=4$ as an example, elements are called I, A, B, C:
    $$
    I={0, 4, -4, 8, -8, \cdots }\
    A={1, 5, -5, 9, -9, \cdots }\
    B={2, 6, -6, 10, -10, \cdots }\
    C={3, 7, -7, 11, -11, \cdots }\
    \text{I is the identity element}
    $$
    That leads to an insight that a group can be described by ‘different’ elements. That may later lead to the notion of isomorphic. In this example, we see however the presentation of the elements in group are changeable in notation, their number will never change. The number of elements in a group is called the order of the group, in chinese, 群的阶

  4. The symmetry group of polygon $D_n$, also called dihedral group, whose elements are all the operation that renders the polygon invariant, that is the rotation $R$ through $\frac{2\pi}{n}$and the reflection $r$ through a median.
    What are the elements in dihedral group?Note that rotation as $R$, namely $R^n = I$and reflection as r, $r^{2} = I$. Clearly, for $R$, we have ${I, R, R^{2}, \cdots, R^{n-1}}$, for the relationship between we have trivally, $rRr=R^{-1} \text{and} r^{-1} = r$, so $(Rr)(Rr)=I$, thus $(R^{k}r)(R^{k}r)=I$, we have ${I, R, R^{2}, \cdots, R^{n-1}, r, Rr, R^{2}r, \cdots , R^{n-1}r}$, it is clear that $D_n$ has $2n$ elements.

Note that each element of $D_n$ permutes the vertices $1, \cdots , n$ of the polygon

  1. The N Nth roots of 1 form the cyclic group $Z_N = {\mathrm{e}^{\frac{i 2\pi j}{N}}:i=0, \cdots , N-1}$. The composition is trivally mulitiplication, and it is abelian.
    cylic group is a kind of special group which is studied completely, we may discuss some of its property later.

  2. Complex number of magnitude 1, namely $\mathrm{e}^{i \phi}$, form a group called $U(1)$. At the level of physicist rigor, we can think of $U(1)$ as the ‘continuum limit’ of $Z_N$ with $ \mathrm{e}^{\frac{i 2\pi j}{N}} \to \mathrm{e}^{i \phi} $ in the limit $ N \to \infin$ and $j \to \infin$ with the ration held fixed $ \frac{2 \pi j}{N}=\phi $

  3. Rotation in 3-dimensional Euiclidean space, is called $SO(3)$, of course $SO(2)$ is to describe rotation in 2-dimension. We suppose here that the determinant of a rotation matrix is equal to 1.

  4. Consider the set of n-by-n matrices $M$ with determinants equal to 1, composing under ordinary matrix mulitiplication. Clearly $\det (M^{-1})=(\det (M))^{-1}=1$, so the inverse of $M$ is in the group, too. Note that if the matrix entries are all complex or real, every element in group is invertible. It is called the real linear group in variables, note as $SL(n, \mathbb{C}) \text{for all the}\ n\times n \ \text{complex matrices}$. If the limitation of determinant is left out, it is called the real general linear group in n variables. They are typically infinte groups. $SO(2) \text{ is subgroup to } SL(2, \mathbb{R})$, $SL(n, \mathbb{R}) \text{ is supgroup to } GL(n, \mathbb{R})$

  5. More commonly, define $O(3)$, of all the orthogonal transformations $A$ in Euiclidean 3-D space, which satisfy
    $$
    |A \vec{v} |=|\vec{v}|
    $$
    If we choose an orthonormal basis for 3-D space so that every $A$ becomes indentified with a matrix, then $A$ is an orthogonal transformation if and only if
    $$
    A A^T= I
    $$
    so, $\det (A)= \pm 1$. So $SO(3)\ \text{is subgroup to }\ O(3)$. Actually $SO(3)$ is called special orthogonal group in 3 variables

1.3 Permutation Group

  • Define elements in $S_n$ as
    $$
    g = \begin{pmatrix}
    1 & 2 &3 &\cdots &n\
    a_1&a_2&a_3&\cdots &a_n\
    \end{pmatrix}
    $$
    which means the map that $i\to a_i$ for $a_i$ is a new permutation of $1, \cdots, n$
  • We can meanwhile define $g$ as $g=(12)(345)$, where $(a_1a_2a_3\cdots a_k)$ is call a k-cycles. They mean the map that $a_1\to a_2\to a_3\to \cdots \to a_1. $
    2-cycles are specially called transpositions or more informally exchanges

Theorem:Any element in $S_n$(excluding $I$) can be uniquely divided into composition of disjoint k-cycles(regardless of the sequence of the k-cycles)
Proof:may leave as an exercise

Having this Theorem, we are now to deal with this question:

Question:Given a cycle structure characterized by the integers $n_j$, determine the number of permutations in $S_n$ with this cycle structure.
Answer:
$$
\mathcal{N}(n_1, \cdots , n_j, \cdots )=\frac{n!}{
\prod_{j}j^{n_j}n_j! }
$$
先排列 $n_j$，除以 $n_j!$，而每个 $j-cycle$中有 $j$种permutation，故再除以 $j^{n_j}$, 最后全部乘起来 -->

Statement:Any permutation can be divided into composition of transpositions.
Proof:Not hard to verify that:
$$
(i_1\ i_2\ \cdots i_n)=(i_1\ i_n)(i_1\ i_{n-1})\cdots (i_1\ i_3)(i_1\ i_2)
$$
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{see that, }&(i_1\ i_n)(i_1\ i_{n-1})\cdots (i_1\ i_3)(i_1\ i_2)\
&=(i_n\ i_1\ i_{n-1})\cdots (i_1\ i_3)(i_1\ i_2)\
&=(i_{n-1}\ i_n\ i_1)(i_1\ i_{n-2})\cdots (i_1\ i_3)(i_1\ i_2)\
&=\cdots \
&=(i_1\ i_2\ \cdots i_n)\
\end{aligned}
$$

Statement:When a permutation is divided into composition of 2-cycles, whether the number of 2-cycles is odd or even will not change.
逆序数：inversion number

equivalence classes

Defination:In a group $G$, two elementS $g$ and $g’$ are said to be equivalent $(g ~ g’)$ if there exists another element $f$ such that
$$
g’=f^{-1}gf
$$

Equivalence is transitive, we can gather all the elements that are all equivalent into equivalence classes.
Consider $A_4$, its equivalence classes are:
$$
{I}, \qquad{(12)(34), (13)(24), (14)(23)}, \
{(123), (142), (134), (243)}, \qquad{(132), (124), (143), (234)}
$$
actually it has a close relationship with tetrahedron(四面体). It we put 1-4 onto the point of tetrahedron, we can see the last two equivalence classes are defined as the right-hand screw principle.
For $S_4$，equivalence classes are:
$$
{I}, \qquad{(12), (13), (14), (23), (24), (34)}\
{(12)(34), (13)(24), (14)(23)}, \
{(123), (142), (134), (243), (132), (124), (143), (234)}\
{(1234), (4231), (3214), (2134), (1423), (1324), (1243), (2143)}
$$

Homomorphisms:the relation between $SL(2, \mathbb{C})$ and the Lorenz group

Homomorphism:Let $\varphi$ be a map from $G_1$ to $G_2$, we say that $\varphi$ is homomorphism if
$$
\varphi(ab)=\varphi(a)\varphi(b)\quad \text{for all a and b in } G_1
$$

Lorentz group:all the operation that makes the Lorentz metric invariant
$$
|\textbf{Bx}|=|\textbf{x}|\
\text{where } |\textbf{x}|^{2} = x_0^{2}-x_1^{2}-x_2^{2}-x_3^{2}\
$$

Let
$$
x=\begin{pmatrix}
x_0+x_3&x_1-ix_2\
x_1+ix_2&x_0-x_3\
\end{pmatrix} \text{represents } x= \begin{pmatrix} x_0 \x_1 \ x_2 \ x_3 \end{pmatrix}\
\det (x)=|\textbf{x}|^{2}
$$
Thus, we turn a 4-D vec into a $2\times 2$ matrix
Note that
$$
x^\dag=x
$$
Actually the matrix is still in a 4-D real vector space, for which a convenient basis consists of the identity
